Africa - Technological leapfrog

Leapfrog – a phrase synonymous with Africa. The potential to overcome persistent challenges in novel ways abound in heaps – partly attributable to a simultaneous resurgence of Africa and a rapid technological boom. Africa has been/is well placed to take advantage of technology and transform itself in a truly unique fashion. This concept extends beyond technology, the focus of this post.

As an example, the proliferation and wild success of mobile phone transformed traditional banking models and prompted thoughts on next generational finance. The targeted investment in clean renewable sources of energy over fossil fuel dependency is another example, happening now! There’s plenty more beyond the scope of this post, I’m sure. Bright B. Simons, on a past post on Harvard Business Review, brilliantly captures my point

“In much the same way that Africa’s lack of significant telecom capacity was a boon rather than a hindrance to the emergence of mobile telephony, its lack of legacy infrastructure for everything ranging from waste management to energy utilities could provide the appetite — non-existent in the West — for genuinely transformative, future-friendly reconceptualization of the very notion of infrastructure” - HBR

Digital currency technology is one of such and is well poised to ‘hack’ some of the present day challenges in Africa.

Tatu Mbao (3 for 20) – Micro Transactions in Informal Economies

Tatu Mbao is a common phrase in Nairobi’s urban informal groceries. Mama mboga (the grocer) an even more popular phrase, sells 3 tomatoes for 20 KES ($0.22) – a classic case of a micro-transaction and micropayment. Ideally, I would imagine myself paying Mama mboga ($0.22) via my phone through some sort of digital currency with close to zero fees.

Micropayments pop up in almost every conversation on virtual currency. Bitcoin and alt coins have reinvigorated the discussion on micropayments for value exchange. There’s been debate on the true definition of a micropayment or micro transaction; Greg Golebiewski sets it straight on this post. Contrary to what PayPal/Visa might have you believe, micro transactions were originally envisioned as transactions of extremely small values; such as Spotify’s $0.000006 royalty fee per stream. Digital currencies have plenty of attributes that suit them for micro transactions - all the qualities of cash, low transfer costs, low friction plus getting rid of all the costs associated with handling cash. Central banks dream of cashless economies. Thoughts of a Central bank issued digital currency loom. The cost advantages are tremendous, along with the possibility of much more effective monetary policies. The Central Bank of Nigeria launched the Nigeria Cashless Project a while back.

“…the introduction of a cashless society empowers central banks greatly. A cashless society, after all, not only makes things like negative interest rates possible, it transfers absolute control of the money supply to the central bank, mostly by turning it into a universal banker that competes directly with private banks for public deposits. All digital deposits become base money.” – Financial Times

Amidst all this, it is easy to forget the informal economy and its extremely low value cash exchanges. Any talk of digital currency should factor in enabling low value transactions in Gikomba (informal market) as well as high end credit card payments at Mr. Price. Talk is cheap - everyone talks about financial inclusion and financial access for the poor and unbanked peoples of developing countries. Mobile money, for instance, is rightly hailed for bringing in the formerly discriminated poor into the formal financial system. Unfortunately, pricing data paints a grim picture of exorbitant transaction fees for small person-to-person value transfer. Of course, key to implementing these solutions will include broader penetration of internet access, internet enabled devices and the user friendliness of applications on these devices to the common folk of the mama mboga caliber. Already, in Nairobi, Kenya, data enabled services such as Whatsapp and FB have gained huge traction. Low cost smartphones have seen massive adoption by consumers. The design criteria of digital currency micro payment applications will have to accommodate intended users; masking the intricacies of its cryptographic operations under a simple design. It’s definitely possible.
